Understanding the Paradox

The term "entry-level" is supposed to mean a role suitable for someone entering the workforce for the first time. In an ideal world, entry-level roles would require zero professional experience. So why do so many listings contradict this?

According to a 2025 LinkedIn Talent Solutions analysis, 61% of job postings labelled "entry-level" include a preference for some prior experience. This does not mean the employer expects a seasoned professional — but it does reveal a gap between what job titles promise and what job descriptions actually ask for. Understanding this gap is the first step to overcoming it.

The 5 Real Reasons Behind the Requirement

Reason 01

It Is Often a "Wish List", Not a Hard Requirement

Many companies list experience requirements because they want the best possible candidate, not because they will actually reject applicants without it. Hiring managers often treat the job description as a wish list of ideal qualifications. If you meet 60–70% of the criteria, you are likely to be considered. A 2023 Harvard Business Review study found that candidates who meet only half the listed qualifications are still invited to interview more than 50% of the time — but only if they apply.

Reason 02

They Want Workplace Readiness, Not Job-Specific Skills

When a company asks for "1–2 years of experience" for an entry-level role, they often mean they want someone who understands basic workplace expectations: showing up on time, communicating professionally, handling feedback, managing deadlines, and working in a team. These are soft skills that can be developed through internships, part-time jobs, volunteer work, or even college committee roles. The employer is not looking for deep industry expertise — they want someone who will not need hand-holding on basic professional behaviour.

Reason 03

HR Departments Use ATS Filters That Screen by Years

Many large companies use Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) to filter resumes before a human ever sees them. Recruiters sometimes set filters for "years of experience" to reduce the volume of applications. This means qualified freshers get automatically rejected — not because the hiring manager would not consider them, but because the software filters them out. The workaround is to network directly with recruiters, apply through alternative channels, or find companies that specifically hire freshers.

Reason 04

The Role May Have Evolved but the JD Has Not

Sometimes the job description (JD) was written years ago and never updated. A role that was genuinely entry-level in 2020 may have evolved to include more responsibilities, but the HR team still uses the old template. The "2–3 years" requirement may simply be a leftover from an earlier version of the role. This is more common than you think, especially in organisations that hire infrequently for that specific position.

Reason 05

Some Companies Want to Pay Entry-Level Salary for Experienced Work

Unfortunately, some employers label a role as "entry-level" to justify a lower salary band while still expecting someone who can hit the ground running. This is a red flag for genuine freshers. However, reputable companies like BTW Group structure their fresher roles with proper training periods, mentorship, and realistic expectations. An honest employer will invest in your growth rather than demand experience you cannot have.

What the Experience Requirement Actually Means

Here is how to decode what companies are really looking for when they list experience in an entry-level JD:

When They Say...

  • "0–1 years" — Genuinely open to freshers
  • "1–2 years" — Prefer some internship/volunteer background
  • "2–3 years" — Want proven workplace readiness, often flexible
  • "3–5 years" — Usually not entry-level; may be misclassified
  • "Experience preferred" — Highly flexible; apply anyway

They Usually Mean...

  • Some exposure to professional environment
  • Basic communication and email etiquette
  • Ability to work independently on routine tasks
  • Familiarity with common tools (MS Office, email, internet research)
  • Understanding of deadlines and accountability

How Freshers Can Overcome This Barrier

The experience paradox is frustrating, but it is not a dead end. Here are five proven strategies to get past the "years of experience" filter:

Strategy 01

Build Experience Through Internships and Freelancing

Even a 2-month internship counts as experience. Platforms like Internshala, LinkedIn, and Upwork offer short-term opportunities that build both skills and resume credibility. Many of our best hires at BTW Group started as interns before transitioning to full-time roles.

Strategy 02

Earn Recognised Certifications

Certifications from Google, Microsoft, HubSpot, or LinkedIn Learning signal to employers that you have invested in your skills. For example, a Google Digital Marketing certification can be as valuable as 6 months of experience for a marketing intern role.

Strategy 03

Leverage College Projects and Volunteer Work

Your college projects, cultural committee roles, NSS volunteering, or part-time tutoring all demonstrate responsibility, time management, and teamwork. Frame them as "experience" on your resume — because they are. Use action verbs and quantify results wherever possible.

Strategy 04

Target Companies That Hire Freshers by Design

Some companies — like BTW Group — structure their hiring specifically for freshers. We provide on-the-job training, mentorship, and a clear growth path. Focusing your applications on such companies saves time and frustration. Look for roles that say "Freshers Welcome" or "0 years experience required".

Strategy 05

Network Directly and Apply Off-Portal

ATS filters are bypassed when you apply through a referral, a career fair, or a direct email to the HR team. Connect with recruiters on LinkedIn, attend job fairs, and use the "Apply Now" link on company career pages rather than job aggregators. A direct application often lands in front of human eyes.

Do's and Don'ts When Applying Without the Required Experience

Do's ✔

  • Apply if you meet 60%+ of the qualifications
  • Write a tailored cover letter explaining your fit
  • Highlight transferable skills from internships, projects, or volunteering
  • Focus on what you can do, not what you lack
  • Follow up after one week if you do not hear back

Don'ts ✘

  • Lie or exaggerate your experience
  • Get discouraged by the number and skip applying
  • Use the same generic resume for every application
  • Blame the "system" — focus on what you can control
  • Ignore small companies — they often hire freshers more readily

Companies vs. Reality: A Fresher's Guide to Reading Job Descriptions

Not all job descriptions are created equal. Learn to read between the lines:

  • If the role says "Entry Level" but asks for 3+ years, it may be misclassified. Look for a different role in the same company, or apply directly to the hiring manager explaining your interest in a fresher capacity.
  • If the role says "Fresher" or "Graduate Trainee", it is genuinely open to candidates with no experience. These are your best targets.
  • If the JD lists "Preferred" instead of "Required", treat it as optional. Apply immediately.
  • If the company has a dedicated fresher hiring page, like BTW Group, that is a strong signal they invest in training new talent.

FAQs About Experience Requirements for Entry-Level Jobs

Why do entry-level jobs ask for 2–3 years of experience?

Many entry-level job listings include an experience requirement as a "wish list" rather than a strict filter. Companies often do this to attract candidates who have basic exposure to workplace dynamics, even though the role itself is designed for freshers. In many cases, the requirement is flexible for the right candidate.

Should I still apply if I don't meet the experience requirement?

Yes. If you meet 60–70% of the listed qualifications, you should apply. Many hiring managers view experience requirements as guidelines, not hard barriers. A strong cover letter, relevant skills, and genuine enthusiasm can often outweigh a lack of formal experience.

How can freshers get experience if no one hires them?

Freshers can build experience through internships, freelancing, volunteering, personal projects, certifications, and campus placements. Even part-time work or managing college events counts as experience when framed correctly on a resume.

Why do some companies genuinely require experience for "entry-level" roles?

Some companies list a role as "entry-level" in terms of pay grade or hierarchy but still require experience because the role involves client-facing responsibilities, handling sensitive data, or working independently from day one. In these cases, they need candidates who can contribute immediately without extensive training.

What can I do if I keep getting rejected for not having experience?

Focus on building transferable skills through internships, certifications (like Google or Microsoft), personal projects, and networking. Tailor your resume for each application, highlight your soft skills, and apply to companies like BTW Group that specifically hire and train freshers.

Does unpaid experience count on a resume?

Yes. Internships, freelance work, volunteer roles, college committee positions, and personal projects all count as experience. What matters is what you learned and accomplished, not whether you were paid. Frame these experiences in terms of skills developed and results achieved.
