Understanding the Backbone of the Global Travel Industry

If you are considering a career in travel, tourism, or air ticketing, you have likely come across the term GDS — Global Distribution System. It sounds technical, but at its core, a GDS is simply the platform that connects travel providers (airlines, hotels, car rentals) with travel sellers (agencies, tour operators, and online booking sites). Think of it as the invisible engine behind almost every flight booking, hotel reservation, and car rental around the world.

At BTW Group, our air ticketing and travel teams use GDS platforms every day to book flights for clients travelling across the globe. In this beginner's guide, we will explain what GDS systems are, how they work, the differences between the three major platforms — Amadeus, Galileo, and Sabre — and which one you should learn to start your travel career in 2026.

What Is a Global Distribution System (GDS)?

A Global Distribution System (GDS) is a centralised technology platform that enables travel professionals to search, compare, book, and manage travel services in real time. It aggregates data from hundreds of airlines, thousands of hotels, car rental companies, and other travel suppliers into a single interface, allowing travel agents to book complex itineraries in minutes.

Before GDS existed, travel agents had to call airlines individually to check availability and make bookings. A single multi-city itinerary could take hours to arrange. GDS revolutionised the industry by bringing all suppliers onto one connected network, making bookings instant, accurate, and efficient.

How a GDS Works

The GDS sits in the middle of the travel booking ecosystem. Here is how a typical transaction flows:

1
Client Request
A traveller asks a travel agent for flights from Mumbai to London on specific dates.
2
GDS Query
The agent enters the request into the GDS (Amadeus, Galileo, or Sabre), which sends a real-time query to airline inventory systems.
3
Availability & Pricing
Airlines respond with seat availability, fare options, and rules. The GDS displays the results within seconds.
4
Booking & Ticketing
The agent selects the best option, creates a PNR (Passenger Name Record), issues the ticket, and sends confirmation to the client.

The Three Major GDS Platforms

While there are several GDS platforms globally, three dominate the market: Amadeus, Galileo (Travelport), and Sabre. Each has its own history, strengths, and regional presence.

Amadeus
Founded: 1987 · Headquarters: Madrid, Spain

Amadeus is the largest GDS platform in the world by market share, handling over 40% of all global travel bookings. It is especially dominant in Europe, Asia, South America, and the Middle East. Amadeus processes more than 1 billion transactions annually and connects over 90,000 travel agencies worldwide.

For the Indian market, Amadeus is the most widely used GDS, making it the top choice for freshers entering the travel industry. Its command-based interface (Amadeus Selling Platform) is powerful and efficient once learned. Amadeus also offers extensive training resources, including the Amadeus Learning Centre and certification programmes recognised globally.

Galileo
Owned by: Travelport · Founded: 1971 (as Apollo), rebranded as Galileo in 1993

Galileo, operated by Travelport, is the second-largest GDS platform globally. It has a strong presence in the United Kingdom, Asia-Pacific, the Middle East, and parts of Europe. Galileo's interface is known for being user-friendly, and it is particularly popular among corporate travel agencies and mid-sized travel firms.

For beginners, Galileo offers a more intuitive graphical interface (Travelport Smartpoint) alongside traditional command entry. This makes it a good starting point for those who find command-line interfaces intimidating. Galileo is widely used in Indian travel agencies, especially those handling corporate travel and outbound tourism.

Sabre
Founded: 1960 · Headquarters: Southlake, Texas, USA

Sabre is the oldest GDS platform, originally created by American Airlines in 1960 as the first computerised reservation system. Today, Sabre is the dominant GDS in North America and has a strong presence in corporate travel management and airline technology solutions. Sabre's interface (Sabre Red Workspace) combines command entry with modern graphical tools.

While Sabre is less common in India compared to Amadeus and Galileo, it is essential for travel agencies and airlines that serve the North American market. If you plan to work for an international airline, a US-based travel company, or a global corporate travel agency, Sabre proficiency is highly valued.

Which GDS Should You Learn as a Beginner?

The short answer: Start with Amadeus. Here is why:

  • Market dominance: Amadeus has the largest global market share and is the most widely used GDS in India, where BTW Group and most travel agencies operate.
  • Training availability: Amadeus offers comprehensive training programmes, including the Amadeus Certified Professional (ACP) certification, which is recognised by employers worldwide.
  • Career flexibility: Once you learn Amadeus, learning Galileo or Sabre becomes much easier because the core concepts — PNR creation, fare calculation, ticketing — are the same across all platforms.

That said, if you are targeting a specific employer or region, check which GDS they use. Many travel agencies in the UK favour Galileo, while North American agencies lean toward Sabre. Knowing two GDS platforms will make you a significantly stronger candidate.

Pro Tip

Many training institutes offer combined Amadeus + Galileo certification programmes for a small additional fee. Investing 3–4 months in dual GDS training can double your job opportunities in the travel industry.

How to Get GDS Certified: A Step-by-Step Guide

1
Choose Your GDS
Start with Amadeus if you are in India, Europe, or Asia. Choose Galileo for UK focus, Sabre for North America.
2
Find a Training Centre
Look for IATA-certified travel institutes. Many offer classroom and online GDS training. Cost: INR 10,000–INR 25,000.
3
Complete Certification
Most programmes run 2–3 months. You will learn flight searches, fare quotes, bookings, ticketing, and cancellations.
4
Practice Daily
GDS proficiency comes from repetition. Practice mock bookings, fare calculations, and PNR modifications regularly.

Is GDS Still Relevant in 2026?

With the rise of online travel aggregators (OTAs) like Booking.com, Expedia, and MakeMyTrip, some wonder if GDS is still necessary. The answer is a clear yes. Here is why:

  • OTAs use GDS: Many online travel platforms actually connect to GDS behind the scenes to access real-time inventory and pricing.
  • Complex bookings: Multi-city itineraries, group bookings, corporate travel policies, and special fare negotiations are handled far more efficiently through GDS than consumer-facing websites.
  • Corporate travel: Large companies manage their travel programmes through GDS-connected corporate travel agencies, ensuring policy compliance, reporting, and negotiated rates.
  • Airlines rely on GDS: Even in 2026, the majority of airline bookings — especially for full-service carriers — flow through GDS channels.

FAQs About GDS Systems

What is a GDS in the travel industry?

A Global Distribution System (GDS) is a centralised network that allows travel agents, airlines, hotels, and other travel providers to book and manage travel services in real time. The three major GDS platforms are Amadeus, Galileo, and Sabre.

Which GDS is best for beginners to learn?

Amadeus is the best GDS for beginners because it has the largest global market share (over 40%), extensive training resources, and is widely used across India, Europe, and Asia. Galileo is also a strong choice for the UK and Asia-Pacific markets.

Is GDS still relevant in 2026?

Yes. Despite the rise of online travel aggregators (OTAs), GDS remains the backbone of the travel industry. Over 60% of airline bookings still go through GDS systems. Airlines, travel agencies, and corporate travel managers continue to rely on GDS for complex bookings, group reservations, and corporate travel management.

How long does it take to learn a GDS?

A basic GDS certification programme typically takes 2 to 3 months of part-time study. With daily practice, most learners become comfortable with core functions — flight searches, fare quotes, bookings, and ticketing — within 4 to 6 weeks.

Do I need a GDS certification to get a travel job?

A GDS certification significantly improves your chances but is not always mandatory. Many employers, including BTW Group, provide GDS training to freshers as part of the onboarding process. However, having a certification before applying gives you a strong advantage over other candidates.

What is the difference between Amadeus, Galileo, and Sabre?

Amadeus is the largest GDS with 40%+ global market share, strongest in Europe, Asia, and South America. Galileo (owned by Travelport) is popular in the UK, Asia-Pacific, and Middle East. Sabre is dominant in North America and has a strong presence in corporate travel management. Each offers similar core functionality but differs in interface, commands, and regional dominance.
